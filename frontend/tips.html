💬 Communication & Presentation

Master the art of clear, confident communication during interviews

3 DO's
2 DON'Ts
5 Total Tips
✅ DO

Maintain appropriate eye contact

Make eye contact 60-70% of the time. Look at the interviewer when they're speaking and when you're making key points.
💡 Example
When answering 'Tell me about yourself,' look at the interviewer while highlighting your key achievements.
✅ DO

Speak clearly and at a moderate pace

Speak slowly enough to be understood, but not so slow that you seem uncertain. Pause between thoughts.
💡 Example
Instead of rushing through your experience, take brief pauses: "I led a team of five developers... [pause] ... and we delivered the project two weeks ahead of schedule."
✅ DO

Practice active listening

Show you're engaged by nodding, asking clarifying questions, and referencing what the interviewer said.
💡 Example
"You mentioned earlier that teamwork is crucial here. In my last role, I collaborated closely with the design team to..."
❌ DON'T

Don't overuse filler words

Minimize "um," "uh," "like," and "you know." It's better to pause briefly than to fill silence with these words.
💡 Example
Instead of: "So, um, like, I worked on, uh, several projects..." Say: "I worked on several key projects... [brief pause] ...including a customer portal redesign."
❌ DON'T

Don't interrupt the interviewer

Let them finish their questions completely. If you need clarification, wait for them to finish, then ask.
💡 Example
Wait for the complete question, then respond: "That's a great question about leadership. Could you clarify what type of leadership scenario you'd like me to focus on?"

💻 Technical Interview Excellence

Navigate technical questions and coding challenges with confidence

3 DO's
2 DON'Ts
5 Total Tips
✅ DO

Think out loud during problem-solving

Verbalize your thought process when tackling technical problems. This shows your problem-solving approach.
💡 Example
"I'm thinking about this as a sorting problem... Let me consider the time complexity... A merge sort would be O(n log n)..."
✅ DO

Ask clarifying questions for technical problems

Before diving into a solution, make sure you understand the requirements, constraints, and expected inputs/outputs.
💡 Example
"Before I start coding, can I clarify: What's the expected size of the input? Are there any memory constraints I should consider?"
✅ DO

Discuss trade-offs in your solutions

Show you understand different approaches by explaining why you chose your solution and what alternatives exist.
💡 Example
"I'm choosing a hash map here for O(1) lookup time, which uses more memory but gives us better performance than a linear search."
❌ DON'T

Don't jump straight into coding

Take time to understand the problem, discuss your approach, and get alignment before writing code.
💡 Example
Instead of immediately coding, say: "Let me make sure I understand this correctly..." and outline your approach first.
❌ DON'T

Don't give up if you're stuck

If you're struggling, explain where you're stuck and ask for hints. Show resilience and problem-solving persistence.
💡 Example
"I'm having trouble optimizing this further. Could you give me a hint about the data structure that might work better here?"

🎯 Behavioral Questions & STAR Method

Master behavioral interviews using proven storytelling frameworks

3 DO's
2 DON'Ts
5 Total Tips
✅ DO

Use the STAR method for behavioral questions

Structure answers with Situation, Task, Action, Result. This provides clear, complete responses to behavioral questions.
💡 Example
Situation: "Our team was behind on a critical project..." Task: "I needed to get us back on track..." Action: "I organized daily standups..." Result: "We delivered on time with 98% quality score."
✅ DO

Prepare 5-7 detailed examples

Have specific stories ready that demonstrate leadership, problem-solving, teamwork, conflict resolution, and achievement.
💡 Example
Prepare stories for: overcoming a challenge, leading a team, dealing with conflict, learning from failure, achieving a goal, working under pressure.
✅ DO

Quantify your results when possible

Use specific numbers, percentages, timeframes to make your impact clear and memorable.
💡 Example
"I reduced processing time by 40%, saved the company $50K annually, and improved customer satisfaction scores from 3.2 to 4.7."
❌ DON'T

Don't give vague or generic answers

Avoid answers like "I'm a team player" without specific examples. Always back up claims with concrete stories.
💡 Example
Instead of "I'm good at problem-solving," say: "When our main server crashed during peak hours, I quickly identified the issue, implemented a workaround, and had us back online in 15 minutes."
❌ DON'T

Don't focus excessively on problems or blame others

Even when discussing challenges, focus on your actions and learnings rather than dwelling on problems or blaming teammates.
💡 Example
Instead of "My manager was terrible and the project was a disaster," say: "I learned to adapt my communication style and found ways to keep the project moving forward despite challenges."

🤝 Body Language & Presence

Project confidence and professionalism through your physical presence

3 DO's
2 DON'Ts
5 Total Tips
✅ DO

Maintain confident, open posture

Sit up straight, keep shoulders back, and avoid crossing arms. Lean slightly forward to show engagement.
💡 Example
Sit with both feet on the floor, hands visible on the table or in your lap, maintaining an open chest position throughout the interview.
✅ DO

Offer a firm, confident handshake

Make eye contact, smile, and give a firm (not crushing) handshake when greeting interviewers.
💡 Example
Stand up, make eye contact, smile genuinely, and say "It's great to meet you" with a confident handshake that matches their grip strength.
✅ DO

Use purposeful hand gestures

Natural hand movements can emphasize points, but keep gestures controlled and purposeful.
💡 Example
When explaining a process, use your hands to show progression or size. When making three points, count them on your fingers.
❌ DON'T

Don't fidget or display nervous habits

Avoid pen clicking, leg bouncing, hair touching, or other repetitive movements that can be distracting.
💡 Example
Keep your hands occupied by holding a portfolio or resting them calmly. If you tend to fidget, practice sitting still during mock interviews.
❌ DON'T

Don't adopt closed or defensive postures

Avoid crossing arms, leaning away, or turning your body away from the interviewer. These signal discomfort or disengagement.
💡 Example
Instead of crossing arms when thinking, place one hand on your chin or keep both hands visible and relaxed.

📚 Interview Preparation & Research

Set yourself up for success with thorough preparation strategies

3 DO's
2 DON'Ts
5 Total Tips
✅ DO

Research the company thoroughly

Study the company's mission, recent news, products, culture, and the specific team you'd be joining.
💡 Example
Read their latest blog posts, annual reports, recent press releases, and employee reviews on Glassdoor. Follow their LinkedIn and social media.
✅ DO

Prepare thoughtful questions to ask

Have 5-7 specific questions ready about the role, team, company culture, growth opportunities, and challenges.
💡 Example
"What does success look like in this role after the first 90 days?" "What are the biggest technical challenges the team is facing?" "How does the company support professional development?"
✅ DO

Practice answers to common questions

Rehearse responses to standard questions, but don't memorize scripts. Practice until your answers flow naturally.
💡 Example
Practice: "Tell me about yourself," "Why do you want this job?" "What's your greatest weakness?" "Where do you see yourself in 5 years?"
❌ DON'T

Don't wing it without preparation

Even if you're highly qualified, lack of preparation shows and can cost you the opportunity.
💡 Example
Don't assume you can "figure it out" during the interview. Spend at least 2-3 hours researching and preparing beforehand.
❌ DON'T

Don't ask generic or easily googled questions

Avoid questions about basic company info that's readily available online. This shows you didn't do your homework.
💡 Example
Don't ask "What does your company do?" or "How big is your team?" Instead, ask about specific challenges or growth plans.
